Calculus

Ordinary arithmetic is happy with things that sit still. But the world doesn't sit still — planets swing, populations grow, prices wobble, a thrown ball curves through the air. Calculus is the mathematics of change and motion, and learning it feels a little like being handed a superpower.

It answers two questions that look impossible at first:

  • How fast, right now? Not your average speed over a whole journey, but your exact speed at a single instant — the steepness of a curve at one precise point. That's the derivative.
  • How much, added up? Slice a curvy, awkward shape into infinitely many infinitely thin pieces, then add them all back together to get an exact area, volume, or total distance. That's the integral.

The wild idea at the heart of it all is the limit — sneaking up on a value by taking steps that get smaller and smaller, forever, and seeing where you land. Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz worked this out (separately, and then argued about it for years), and it turned out to be the language the universe is written in: it flies rockets, designs bridges, prices options, models epidemics, and trains the AI reading these words.

We'll build it up one small, friendly step at a time — no leaps. The very first step isn't even calculus yet; it's making sure we're rock-solid on what a function is.
