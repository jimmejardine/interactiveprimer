Calculus

Ordinary arithmetic is happy with things that sit still. But the world doesn't sit still — planets swing, populations grow, prices wobble, a thrown ball curves through the air. Calculus is the mathematics of change and motion, built on a single audacious idea: that you can tame the infinite by sneaking up on it with limits.

It answers two questions that look impossible at first — and then reveals they are two sides of one coin:

  • How fast, right now? The exact rate of change at a single instant — the steepness of a curve at one precise point. That's the derivative.
  • How much, added up? Slice a curvy region into infinitely many infinitely thin pieces and add them back to an exact total — area, volume, distance, probability. That's the integral.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is the thunderclap that says these two are inverses of one another. From there the subject opens out — to infinite series, to calculus in many dimensions, to the calculus of vector fields, to differential equations, and finally to the rigorous foundations of real analysis that make all of it watertight.

The shape of the journey

This is a full course, from first principles to masters level. It moves in seven parts, each building on the last.

  • Part I — Differential calculus. Functions, limits, the derivative, the rules for computing it, and what it tells you about a curve.
  • Part II — Integral calculus. The definite integral, the Fundamental Theorem, techniques of integration, and what areas and volumes it computes.
  • Part III — Sequences & series. Infinite sums, when they converge, and how Taylor series turn functions into polynomials.
  • Part IV — Multivariable calculus. Partial derivatives, gradients, and integration over regions in two and three dimensions.
  • Part V — Vector calculus. Line and surface integrals and the great theorems of Green, Stokes and Gauss.
  • Part VI — Differential equations. Equations for an unknown function, and how to solve the most important families.
  • Part VII — Real analysis. The rigorous foundations — completeness, ε–δ, uniform convergence, and the Riemann integral done properly.

Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz worked out the heart of it (and then argued about it for years); the rigour came later, from Cauchy, Riemann and Weierstrass. We'll build it all one small, friendly step at a time — no leaps.

Beyond — The calculus of variations

One last summit. Instead of optimising over numbers, we optimise over whole functions: which curve is shortest, which slide is fastest, which chain hangs with least energy? A functional turns a curve into a number, and the Euler–Lagrange equation is the "derivative equals zero" of this richer world — the language behind least action, optics and general relativity.
