Partial Differential Equations

An ordinary differential equation tells you how one quantity changes along one variable — a population P(t) growing over time, a cooling cup's temperature T(t) falling minute by minute. One input, one output, one rate of change.

The real world is rarely that tidy. A metal plate's temperature depends on where you probe it and when. A guitar string's height depends on position along the string and time. The unknown is now a function of several variables at once, u(x, t), and the law that governs it is a partial differential equation (PDE) — an equation relating partial derivatives of u with respect to more than one variable.

That single shift — one variable to several — is why PDEs turn out to be the mathematical language of nearly all of physics: heat flow, sound, light, quantum mechanics, fluid dynamics, the shape of a drum skin, the bending of a bridge. Wherever something varies smoothly across space and changes over time, a PDE is quietly running the show.

Reading the notation

We write partial derivatives with a subscript shorthand. If u(x, t) is the temperature at position x and time t, then

u_t = \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}, \qquad u_x = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}, \qquad u_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2},

and so on — each subscript is one more partial derivative with respect to that variable, holding the others fixed. A PDE is simply an equation that relates several of these together, such as

u_t = \alpha\, u_{xx}.

Read left to right: "how fast the temperature at a point is changing in time" equals "a constant times how curved the temperature profile is in space, right at that point." Two different kinds of derivative, two different variables, tied together in one law — that entanglement is the entire character of a PDE, and it's what makes them so much richer (and trickier) than ODEs.

Worked example: the heat equation, informally

Take a metal rod, heat a patch in the middle, and watch. The temperature u(x, t) obeys the heat equation,

u_t = \alpha\, u_{xx}, \qquad \alpha > 0.

Read it as a rule about curvature. At a point where the temperature profile bulges upward (a hot spike, concave down, so u_{xx} < 0), that point is hotter than its immediate neighbours, so heat drains away and u_t < 0 — it cools. At a dip (concave up, u_{xx} > 0), the point is colder than its neighbours, so heat flows in and u_t > 0 — it warms. Curvature drives change. That is why a sharp hot spike smooths itself into a gentle bump, and the bump slowly flattens towards a uniform temperature — drag the slider below and watch a hot bump do exactly that.

A useful special case: if the profile is a perfectly straight line, it has no curvature at all, so u_{xx} = 0 and therefore u_t = 0 — a straight temperature ramp never changes shape under the heat equation. Only bends and bumps drive the evolution. We'll derive this equation properly, and solve it exactly, in the heat equation.

Worked example: the wave equation, informally

Pluck a guitar string and its height u(x, t) above the rest position obeys a different law, the wave equation,

u_{tt} = c^2\, u_{xx}.

This time it's the acceleration in time, u_{tt}, that answers to curvature in space. Where the string bulges away from its neighbours (curved), tension yanks it back — the more sharply it's bent, the harder the tug. That restoring tug is what makes the string overshoot, spring back, and overshoot again: an oscillation that travels along the string as a wave, rather than smoothing away like heat does. Same ingredient — spatial curvature — but paired with acceleration instead of a first-time-derivative, and the behaviour it produces could not look more different. We meet this equation properly, and its beautifully simple travelling-wave solutions, in the wave equation.

Why PDEs are hard: there is no master method

Most of the ODEs you meet early on fall into a short list of recognisable shapes — separable, linear-first-order, and so on — each with a dependable recipe that always works. PDEs don't cooperate like that. The heat equation, the wave equation, and Laplace's equation all look superficially similar — a handful of partial derivatives set equal to one another — yet they describe utterly different physical behaviour (smoothing versus oscillating versus a steady balance), and each one genuinely needs its own techniques.

That is the shape of the journey ahead: rather than one universal solving trick, this unit builds up a toolbox piece by piece — classifying a PDE by its order and linearity, exploiting superposition where it applies, following characteristics for first-order equations, separating variables for the heat and wave equations, and falling back on numerical methods when no exact formula is available at all. Learning which tool fits which equation is most of the game.

A single PDE like u_t = \alpha\, u_{xx} does not pin down one answer — it describes an entire family of functions that all satisfy the relationship. Add any constant to a solution of Laplace's equation, or shift a wave-equation solution sideways in time, and you often get another valid solution to the very same equation.

What narrows a whole family down to the one function that matches your actual rod, your actual string, your actual room, is a set of extra facts bolted onto the PDE: the temperature you started with, the temperature held fixed at the two ends, the shape you plucked the string into. These are the boundary and initial conditions, and skipping them is the single most common way to misjudge what a PDE is even asking. Pretty much, yes. Newton's law of cooling grows into the heat equation. A vibrating string, a sound wave in air, and a beam of light are all, underneath, the wave equation in disguise. The steady electric potential around a charged wire solves Laplace's equation. Quantum mechanics runs on the Schrödinger equation; general relativity's account of gravity is Einstein's field equations — a PDE (well, ten coupled ones) for the geometry of spacetime itself.

From a schoolboy watching an apple fall to a physicist bending spacetime around a black hole, the equations describing how the universe changes from point to point and moment to moment are, again and again, PDEs. They are quite literally the language physics is written in — which is exactly why it's worth learning to read it.
