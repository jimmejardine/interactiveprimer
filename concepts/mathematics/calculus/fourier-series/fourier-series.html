Fourier Series

Here is one of the most far-reaching ideas in all of mathematics: almost any function can be built out of sines and cosines. Feed in a jagged square wave, a saw-toothed ramp, the profile of a plucked string — and each is just a sum of smooth ripples of different frequencies, stacked in the right amounts.

f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\Big(a_n\cos\frac{n\pi x}{L} + b_n\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\Big).

Joseph Fourier made the claim in 1822 while studying how heat flows, and his contemporaries flatly disbelieved it — how could ripples ever add up to a sharp corner? They can, and this course shows exactly how and why.

The big idea, in plain language

Start with any periodic wiggle — a shape that repeats itself forever, tick after tick, like a heartbeat or a musical note. Fourier's claim is that this repeating shape is always a sum of one basic sine wave (the fundamental, ticking at the same repeat-rate as the original) plus a stack of faster sine and cosine waves called harmonics — the 2nd harmonic wiggles twice as fast as the fundamental, the 3rd wiggles three times as fast, and so on forever.

Each harmonic gets its own personal amplitude — how tall or loud that particular wiggle is allowed to be. Some harmonics might barely contribute at all (amplitude close to zero); others might dominate the shape. Add every harmonic together, each scaled by its own amplitude, and — however strange the original shape was — you rebuild it exactly. The one missing piece of the story is how to find each harmonic's amplitude; that's exactly what the Fourier coefficients compute.

Worked example: watching a square wave get built, harmonic by harmonic

A square wave — flat at +1, then an instant jump down to -1, back and forth forever — looks nothing like a sine curve. Yet it's built entirely out of sines. Its Fourier series turns out to use only the odd harmonics, each shrinking in amplitude as 1/k:

\text{square}(x) \approx \frac{4}{\pi}\left(\sin x + \frac{\sin 3x}{3} + \frac{\sin 5x}{5} + \frac{\sin 7x}{7} + \cdots\right).

Use the control below to add harmonics one at a time and watch a plain sine curve fold itself into sharper and sharper corners, chasing the flat-topped square wave.

With just the fundamental, you get a plain sine wave — a rough guess at best. Add the 3rd harmonic and the curve already grows flatter shoulders and a steeper middle crossing; it's clearly leaning towards a square shape. Keep adding odd harmonics forever and the fit becomes essentially perfect everywhere except right at the jumps, where a small overshoot stubbornly refuses to shrink away — a famous wrinkle called the Gibbs phenomenon that you'll meet properly further down the path.

Real example: why a violin and a flute sound different

Play the same musical note — say, concert A — on a flute and a violin, tuned to exactly the same pitch (the same fundamental frequency). They don't sound alike at all, and Fourier series explain precisely why. Neither instrument produces a pure sine wave; each produces that shared fundamental plus its own particular blend of harmonics, every one with its own amplitude.

The flute's tone sits close to a pure sine wave: the fundamental dominates and the harmonics are faint, giving a smooth, breathy sound. The violin's tone packs in strong harmonics right up into the higher numbers, giving it that rich, buzzy edge. That distinctive mixture of harmonic amplitudes is called timbre (say "TAM-br") — it's the reason you can tell instruments apart on the radio even when they play the exact same note at the exact same loudness. Change the harmonic amplitudes, in effect, and you change the instrument.

A Fourier series works this cleanly only because it assumes the function is genuinely periodic — it repeats forever, tick after tick, forwards and backwards. Feed in a one-off signal that never repeats — a single hand-clap, a single flash of lightning — and a classic Fourier series has no natural "period" to lock onto. That doesn't mean Fourier's idea fails; it means you need a close cousin built for exactly this case, the Fourier transform, which swaps the discrete stack of harmonics for a continuous blend of every possible frequency. Keep the two straight: a series for repeating patterns, a transform for one-off signals.

Fourier wasn't chasing an abstract theorem when he dreamed all this up — he was trying to describe how heat spreads through a solid metal bar over time, work he pursued while serving as a government administrator in Grenoble (a post Napoleon himself had assigned him). Solving that heat equation is what forced him to claim that any temperature profile along the bar could be broken into sine waves — a claim so bold that Lagrange, one of the greatest mathematicians alive at the time, argued against it in front of the French Academy of Sciences and helped delay its publication for years. Fourier turned out to be essentially right (with some technical fine print later mathematicians had to tidy up), and a problem about cooling metal ended up reshaping signal processing, image compression, quantum mechanics, and practically every corner of modern engineering.

Why it matters

The trick that makes it all work is orthogonality: sines and cosines of different frequencies are "perpendicular" in the same precise sense that dot-product axes are perpendicular. That turns "find the right amounts" into a mechanical projection — one integral per coefficient.

Once you can take a function apart into frequencies, you can solve equations one frequency at a time. That is the engine behind the separation of variables method for the heat, wave, and Laplace equations — which is exactly why we build this toolkit first.
