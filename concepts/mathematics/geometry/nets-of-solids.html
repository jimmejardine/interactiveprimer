Nets of Solids

Unfold the box

Take a cardboard cereal box, snip along some edges, and flatten it out. What you are left with is a net — the 3D shape opened up into one flat piece. Every cardboard box, drinks carton, and flat-pack shelf you have ever owned started life as a net: a flat outline that folds up into the solid.

A net is wonderfully useful because it shows all the faces at once, laid side by side where you can see and measure them. That makes it the perfect tool for working out a solid's surface area or for building a model from card.

One piece per face

A net is the flat 2D shape you get when you unfold a solid and lay all its faces out side by side, joined along their edges. Fold the net back up and you get the solid again. Each face of the solid appears exactly once in the net, so the number of pieces in the net equals the number of faces.

For example:

  • a cube unfolds into 6 squares (often arranged in a cross or a T shape);
  • a cuboid unfolds into 6 rectangles, in 3 matching pairs;
  • a triangular prism unfolds into 2 triangles and 3 rectangles;
  • a cylinder unfolds into 2 circles and 1 rectangle (the rectangle wraps round to make the curved side).
A net folds up into the solid, with one flat piece per face:
  • the number of pieces in the net = the number of faces of the solid;
  • a cube has a net of 6 squares — and there are 11 different valid cube nets;
  • a triangular prism has a net of 2 triangles + 3 rectangles;
  • the surface area of a solid is the total area of its net.

Worked examples

1) Which nets fold into a cube? Six squares can be arranged many ways, but not all of them close up into a cube. A straight line of six squares (1 \times 6) cannot — as you fold, the far squares crash into each other and leave the cube open. The classic cross (a column of four with one square on each side of the second) folds perfectly. Of all the arrangements of six joined squares, exactly 11 are valid cube nets.

2) Surface area of a cuboid from its net. A cuboid measures 5 \times 3 \times 2 cm. Unfolded, its net has 6 rectangles in 3 matching pairs. Add the area of every face:

2(5\times 3) + 2(5\times 2) + 2(3\times 2) = 30 + 20 + 12 = 62\ \text{cm}^2.

Reading the areas straight off a net is exactly why nets make surface area easy — no hidden faces to forget.

3) Sketching a net. To draw the net of a triangular prism, start with a row of 3 rectangles (the long side faces) and attach a triangle to the top and bottom of the middle rectangle (the two ends) — 5 pieces in all. For a square-based pyramid, draw 1 square in the middle and fold a triangle up from each of its four edges — 1 square + 4 triangles = 5 pieces.

Two traps snare almost everyone with nets:

  • Not every arrangement of six squares folds into a cube. Many of them overlap or leave a gap when you fold — only 11 of the possible arrangements actually close up. Before you trust a "cube net", mentally fold it and check no two faces clash and no hole is left open.
  • Count every face for surface area. A cuboid has 6 faces in 3 matching pairs — it is fatally easy to draw only the 3 you can see and forget the 3 on the back, top and far side. Missing a hidden face is the number-one surface-area error.

Nets are how the physical world gets packaged. Every drinks carton, pizza box and flat-pack wardrobe is designed as a flat net first — printed and cut from a single sheet, then folded up — because a flat sheet is cheap to print and stacks with almost no wasted material. Designers play a real game of geometry, tiling nets edge-to-edge so the cutting machine throws away as little card as possible.

The same "which flat shape folds into which solid?" question reaches into deep mathematics. Topology studies exactly how surfaces can be cut and folded; engineers use clever fold patterns (some borrowed from origami) to pack huge structures into tiny spaces — a satellite's solar panels launch folded flat and unfold like a net once in orbit. Even proteins, the tiny machines in your cells, work by folding a long flat chain into a precise 3D shape. Unfolding and refolding turns out to be one of nature's favourite tricks.
