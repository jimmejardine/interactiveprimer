Number Theory

Number theory is the study of the whole numbers — 1, 2, 3, \dots — and the astonishingly deep patterns hiding inside them. It asks the most childlike questions in all of mathematics ("which numbers are prime?", "when is one number a multiple of another?", "which sums of squares are possible?") and discovers that the answers run breathtakingly deep, connecting to almost every other branch of the subject.

Carl Friedrich Gauss called it the queen of mathematics. Its questions are easy to state and often fiendishly hard to answer — some, like the Riemann Hypothesis, have resisted the finest minds for over a century. And for two thousand years it was prized as the purest, most useless of mathematics — until it quietly became the machinery that keeps every online secret safe.

The big idea: divisibility and primes

One thread runs through the whole subject: divisibility — which numbers divide which. From it grow the primes, the indivisible atoms every other number is built from, and the whole edifice of modular arithmetic, the "clock arithmetic" of remainders. Master those two ideas and the rest of number theory unfolds as a series of ever-more-surprising consequences.

The shape of the journey

The course climbs in nine stages, each resting on the last.

  • Stage 1 — Divisibility & the integers. What it means to divide: the division algorithm, greatest common divisors, Euclid's algorithm and Bézout's identity.
  • Stage 2 — Primes. The atoms of arithmetic: unique factorisation, why the primes never run out, how to sieve and test for them, and how they thin out.
  • Stage 3 — Congruences. Clock arithmetic: working modulo n, solving congruences, inverses, and the Chinese Remainder Theorem.
  • Stage 4 — The multiplicative world. Fermat, Euler and the totient function; the order of an element, primitive roots, and the discrete logarithm.
  • Stage 5 — Quadratic residues. Which numbers are squares mod p — the Legendre symbol and the jewel of the subject, quadratic reciprocity.
  • Stage 6 — Arithmetic functions. Counting divisors, perfect numbers, the Möbius function and the inversion that ties them together.
  • Stage 7 — Diophantine equations. Whole-number solutions: Pythagorean triples, sums of two squares, the Gaussian integers, continued fractions and Pell's equation.
  • Stage 8 — Analytic number theory. Calculus meets the primes: the Riemann zeta function, the Prime Number Theorem, and the Riemann Hypothesis.
  • Stage 9 — Algebraic number theory. Numbers beyond the integers: number fields, ideals, and how unique factorisation is rescued when it breaks.

Going deeper — a master's course on the primes

Stage 8 gives the pictures of how the primes are distributed. To see the proofs — and to follow the story all the way to twenty-first-century research — there is a full graduate course, Analytic Number Theory: the rigorous Prime Number Theorem, Dirichlet L-functions, the zeros of zeta, sieve methods, bounded gaps between primes, the circle method and the automorphic frontier.
