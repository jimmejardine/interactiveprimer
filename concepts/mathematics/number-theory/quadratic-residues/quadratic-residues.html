Quadratic Residues

Every non-negative real number has a square root — 9 has 3, 2 has an ugly decimal, but something always squares to it. Clock arithmetic is stingier. Working \bmod\ n, only some numbers are perfect squares — there may be no x at all with x^{2} \equiv a \pmod n. A number that is such a square is called a quadratic residue; one that isn't is a non-residue.

This sounds like a small bookkeeping question, but it isn't. Deciding "is a a square mod n?" turns out to be exactly the kind of yes/no puzzle that is cheap to check once you know the answer (just square the witness) but expensive to search for by brute force when n is enormous. That lopsidedness — easy to verify, hard to find — is catnip for cryptography, and a compact piece of notation, the Legendre symbol, will soon let us answer the question without ever brute-force checking every candidate.

Definition

We can add, subtract, multiply and even divide modulo n. Squaring is just repeated multiplication, so it makes sense to ask which residues are squares.

Let n be a positive integer and a an integer coprime to n.

  • a is a quadratic residue modulo n if x^{2} \equiv a \pmod n has a solution x.
  • If no such x exists, a is a quadratic non-residue.

Notice the definition only talks about residues coprime to n — we're asking about the multiplicative structure, so numbers sharing a factor with n (and especially 0 itself) are usually set aside as special cases rather than counted among the "interesting" residues or non-residues.

Why insist on coprimality? Because once a shares a factor with n, multiplication by a stops being reversible modulo n — there's no modular inverse to undo it, and the tidy "residues split into two equal camps that multiply like signs" picture (coming up shortly) breaks down. Restricting to residues coprime to n keeps every nonzero class invertible, which is exactly the setting where quadratic residues behave so predictably.

Worked example: squares modulo 7

The cleanest way to find every quadratic residue modulo a small prime is simply to square every nonzero residue and reduce. Modulo 7 the nonzero residues are 1,2,3,4,5,6, so:

1^2\equiv 1,\ \ 2^2\equiv 4,\ \ 3^2\equiv 2,\ \ 4^2\equiv 2,\ \ 5^2\equiv 4,\ \ 6^2\equiv 1 \pmod 7.

Collecting the distinct results gives the residues \{1, 2, 4\} and leaves \{3, 5, 6\} as non-residues — a clean three-three split of the six nonzero classes. Notice also that each square shows up twice in the list: 1 comes from both 1 and 6, 4 comes from both 2 and 5, and 2 comes from both 3 and 4. That doubling is not a coincidence — it's the engine behind the next theorem.

Worked example: squares modulo 13

The same doubling pattern appears at every prime. Modulo 13, square each of the twelve nonzero residues:

1^2, 12^2 \equiv 1; \quad 2^2, 11^2 \equiv 4; \quad 3^2, 10^2 \equiv 9; \quad 4^2, 9^2 \equiv 3; \quad 5^2, 8^2 \equiv 12; \quad 6^2, 7^2 \equiv 10 \pmod{13}.

Twelve numbers collapse onto just six distinct squares: \{1, 3, 4, 9, 10, 12\}. The remaining six residues, \{2, 5, 6, 7, 8, 11\}, are non-residues. Look closely at the pairings above — 1 pairs with 12 = 13 - 1, 2 pairs with 11 = 13 - 2, and so on. Every pair adds up to 13. That's the general pattern: x and p - x (which is the same as -x modulo p) always square to the same value, because (p-x)^{2} = p^{2} - 2px + x^{2} \equiv x^{2} \pmod p.

Worked example: the pattern repeats at 17

One more prime makes the pattern impossible to miss. Modulo 17 the nonzero residues run from 1 to 16 — 16 of them. Squaring gives:

1^2, 16^2 \equiv 1; \ \ 2^2, 15^2 \equiv 4; \ \ 3^2, 14^2 \equiv 9; \ \ 4^2, 13^2 \equiv 16; \ \ 5^2, 12^2 \equiv 8; \ \ 6^2, 11^2 \equiv 2; \ \ 7^2, 10^2 \equiv 15; \ \ 8^2, 9^2 \equiv 13 \pmod{17}.

That's eight pairs producing eight distinct squares: \{1, 2, 4, 8, 9, 13, 15, 16\} — exactly \tfrac{17-1}{2} = 8 residues out of 16. The table below lines up all three examples so the pattern is unmistakable:

  • p = 7: 6 nonzero residues, 3 are squares.
  • p = 13: 12 nonzero residues, 6 are squares.
  • p = 17: 16 nonzero residues, 8 are squares.

Every single time, precisely half. That regularity is strong enough to deserve a name and a proof — not just a pattern we noticed three times in a row.

Exactly half are squares

For an odd prime p, exactly \tfrac{p-1}{2} of the p-1 nonzero residues are quadratic residues, and the other \tfrac{p-1}{2} are non-residues.

Both worked examples above illustrate exactly why this is true. The pairing x \leftrightarrow p - x matches up the p-1 nonzero residues into \tfrac{p-1}{2} pairs that square to the same value, and different pairs give different squares (this takes a short extra argument to nail down completely, but the examples make it very believable). So the p-1 nonzero residues collapse two-to-one onto \tfrac{p-1}{2} distinct quadratic residues — modulo 7 that's 3 residues out of 6, and modulo 13 that's 6 out of 12. Always exactly half.

A multiplication rule

Residues and non-residues multiply like signs — like + and -:

\text{R}\cdot\text{R} = \text{R}, \quad \text{R}\cdot\text{N} = \text{N}, \quad \text{N}\cdot\text{N} = \text{R}.

Check it modulo 7, where the residues are \{1,2,4\} and the non-residues are \{3,5,6\}. Take the residue 2 and the non-residue 3: 2 \times 3 = 6, and 6 is indeed a non-residue — matching \text{R}\cdot\text{N} = \text{N}. Now multiply two non-residues, 3 and 5: 3 \times 5 = 15 \equiv 1 \pmod 7, and 1 is a residue — matching \text{N}\cdot\text{N} = \text{R}.

This sign-like behaviour begs for a compact notation that captures it in one symbol — the Legendre symbol, next. It also gives a quick way to spot a residue or non-residue without consulting a table: if you already know a handful of small residues and non-residues, you can multiply your way to the status of many other numbers. Knowing that 2 is a residue and 3 is a non-residue modulo 7, for instance, instantly tells you 2 \times 3 = 6 must be a non-residue and 2 \times 2 = 4 must be a residue — no squaring required for either.

Why bother? Testing without brute force

For a small modulus like 7 or 13, "is a a residue?" is answered in seconds by squaring everything and looking. But in cryptography, moduli are numbers hundreds of digits long — squaring every candidate would take longer than the age of the universe. We need a way to decide residue-or-not directly, without ever listing all the squares. The multiplication rule above is the first clue that such a shortcut exists: residues and non-residues behave so predictably under multiplication that the whole question can be turned into simple arithmetic, which is precisely what the Legendre symbol does next.

To feel the scale of the problem, imagine a modulus with just 100 digits — small by cryptographic standards (real systems like RSA commonly use moduli of around 600 decimal digits). Listing every nonzero residue and squaring it would mean performing roughly 10^{100} multiplications: far more operations than there are atoms in the observable universe, and utterly impossible for any computer that will ever be built. Whatever shortcut we find has to answer the question using only a handful of arithmetic steps — tens or hundreds, not 10^{100}.

The clean half-and-half split is a special gift of prime moduli — don't assume it elsewhere.

  • The theorem says exactly half of the nonzero residues modulo an odd prime are quadratic residues. For a composite modulus the count can be very different: modulo 8, for instance, only 1 is a nonzero quadratic residue among the units — nowhere near half.
  • 0 is a special case that people often mishandle. Since 0^{2} \equiv 0, zero is technically always a square — but it's excluded from the "coprime to n" residues and from the half-half count, because it doesn't share in the multiplicative structure that makes the residue/non-residue split interesting. When a problem says "how many quadratic residues modulo p?" it almost always means the \tfrac{p-1}{2} nonzero ones, not \tfrac{p-1}{2}+1 with zero thrown in.

Quadratic residues are not just theoretical tidiness — they underpin real cryptographic tricks. The Rabin cryptosystem encrypts by squaring modulo a product of two large primes, and its security rests on the fact that deciding whether a number is a quadratic residue modulo a composite n is exactly as hard as factoring n itself — nobody knows a shortcut.

Even more delightfully, quadratic residues solve a puzzle that sounds impossible: two people who don't trust each other, talking only over a phone line, want to flip a fair coin. One classic protocol has one party pick a large n = pq and a random number, and the other guesses whether that number is a quadratic residue modulo n — a guess that can be checked afterwards but not predicted beforehand, exactly like a coin landing heads or tails. No trusted referee required, just arithmetic.

The trick works because, for a composite n = pq whose factorization is secret, telling residues and non-residues apart is believed to be just as hard as factoring n itself. Whoever announced the number knows the factors (so knows the true answer), while the guesser is stuck with genuine 50/50 odds — the same half-and-half split you just proved for prime moduli shows up again, one level up, in the security of the protocol.
