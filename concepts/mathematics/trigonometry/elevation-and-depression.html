Angles of Elevation and Depression

Trigonometry isn't just for triangles on paper — it answers real "how high?" and "how far?" questions. The key is the line of sight from an observer to an object, and the angle it makes with the horizontal.

  • The angle of elevation is measured up from the horizontal — the angle you tilt your gaze to look up at a treetop or the top of a building.
  • The angle of depression is measured down from the horizontal — the angle you tilt your gaze to look down from a cliff or a window at something below.

When two people look at each other — you up at the cliff-top, someone down at the boat — the angle of elevation from below equals the angle of depression from above. The two horizontals are parallel, so these are alternate angles between parallel lines.

To solve a problem, draw a right-angled triangle: the horizontal distance, the vertical height, and the slanted line of sight. Then it's just SOH-CAH-TOA again.

For an observer looking at an object across a horizontal:
  • the angle of elevation is the angle measured up from the horizontal;
  • the angle of depression is the angle measured down from the horizontal;
  • between the same two points the two angles are equal (alternate angles between parallel horizontals);
  • model the situation with a right-angled triangle and solve with \sin, \cos or \tan.
