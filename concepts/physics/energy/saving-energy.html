Saving Energy

Nearly all the energy in your home has come a long way, and it hasn't been free. Most of it starts as a fuel being burned somewhere — gas, oil, coal — to warm your radiators, heat your water and light up your rooms. Burning that fuel costs money, and the smoke it makes is not kind to our planet.

So here is one of the cleverest, kindest habits you can pick up, and it costs you nothing: don't let energy leak away. You don't have to go without warmth or light — you just stop the energy trickling off when nobody is using it. That is what saving energy really means.

Where energy leaks away at home

A warm, bright house is a bit like a bucket with slow leaks in it. The energy keeps pouring in, and it keeps sneaking out again — usually without anyone noticing. The main leaks are:

  • Heat escaping through cold windows, gaps under doors and up through the roof.
  • Lights left on in empty rooms, glowing away for no one.
  • Hot taps running — every drop of hot water took energy to heat, and it's gurgling straight down the drain.
  • Gadgets left on standby — the telly, the console, the speaker, all still sipping energy while you think they're off.

None of these leaks is dramatic. That's exactly why they matter — a hundred tiny trickles, all day and all night, add up to a great deal of wasted fuel.

There is a special camera — a thermal camera — that doesn't see colours, it sees heat. Point it at a house on a cold winter's night and something startling appears: the building lights up in fiery oranges and reds exactly where warmth is escaping. The windows blaze. A hot patch glows around the front door. The roof shimmers where heat is floating up and out into the sky.

A well-looked-after house shows up mostly cool and blue, because its warmth is staying inside where it belongs. The picture makes something invisible suddenly obvious: heat is always trying to escape, and every glowing patch is money and fuel leaking into the night.

Simple ways to plug the leaks

The good news is that stopping the leaks is easy. None of these needs a grown-up expert or a single coin — mostly they just need someone to remember:

  • Switch things off when you leave — the light, the telly, the tap.
  • Close doors behind you so the warm air stays in the room instead of wandering off down the hall.
  • Feeling cold? Put on a jumper before you reach for the heating.
  • Walk or cycle for a short trip instead of climbing into a car that burns fuel.
  • Help the house keep its warmth — thick curtains, a draught snake by the door, insulation stuffed into the loft. That's called insulating, and it's like giving the house a cosy coat.

Look around a dark living room at night and you'll spot them: tiny red and green lights glowing on the telly, the game console, the microwave, the speaker. Those little glows are a giveaway. The gadget isn't really off — it's on standby, dozing with one eye open, quietly sipping energy so it can wake up the instant you press a button.

People sometimes call this "phantom" power, because it haunts the house all night using energy for nothing. One gadget on standby is only a trickle — but a whole home full of them, every night of the year, adds up to a surprising pile of wasted fuel. A phone charger left plugged in with no phone on it does the very same thing. If you're truly finished, switch it off at the wall, and the phantom vanishes.

Have a go: find the leaks

Here is a little room with three things left running — a light, a heater and a tap — while nobody is using them. The tall bar on the right is the energy wasted meter. Turn each thing off and watch its glow, warmth or water fade, and the waste bar drop. Switch everything off and the waste falls all the way to nothing.

  • You can't really "use up" energy. When we say energy is used up, it hasn't vanished — it has spread out as a gentle, low-grade warmth into the room, the walls and the air, so thinly that we can't easily gather it back and use it again. That's the real reason saving matters: once energy has spread out, it's gone for us.
  • "Off" isn't always off. A red standby light means the gadget is still drawing energy. To truly stop it, switch it off at the wall.
  • Turning the heating up doesn't warm a room faster. The heater pours out warmth at its own steady pace either way — cranking the dial up just makes the room end up hotter, wasting more fuel. It doesn't get you cosy any quicker.

The clever secret: "saved" energy is wasted energy you didn't spend

Here's the surprising part. When you switch off a light in an empty room, you haven't made any new energy — you can't. What you've done is not waste the energy that would have leaked away for nothing. The energy you "save" is simply the energy you were about to throw away, kept safely unburned instead.

That's why every small habit counts. A jumper instead of the heating, a closed door, a tap turned off, a gadget switched off at the wall — each one is a leak plugged. Add them all up, across every home, and it becomes a mountain of fuel that never had to be burned, money that never had to be spent, and a planet that breathes a little easier. Don't let it leak away.
