Air and Water Resistance

Wave your hand slowly through the air and you feel almost nothing. Now swish it fast, or push it through a bowl of water, and you feel something pushing back. Air and water are made of tiny bits, and to move through them you have to shove them out of the way — so they shove back on you. That back-push is called resistance, or drag.

You feel more drag when two things happen:

  • You are bigger and flatter — a wide shape has to push aside more air or water.
  • You are going faster — the quicker you move, the harder it pushes back.

That is exactly why a parachute works. A falling person has their weight pulling them straight down. Open a big parachute and its wide canopy catches masses of air — so the air pushes up hard, drag grows, and the fall becomes gentle and slow instead of fast and dangerous.

Drag the slider to change the size of the parachute. A tiny canopy catches barely any air, so the up-push is small and the weight wins — down you plummet. Make the canopy big and the air push (the up-arrows) grows, holding you up so you drift down gently.

Cutting through, or catching it

  • A racing cyclist crouches down small and narrow. With less of them facing the wind, the air has less to push against, so they slip through and go faster.
  • Try to run in a swimming pool and you can hardly move. Water is much thicker than air, so it pushes back far more — walking through water is slow and heavy work.

Smooth, pointy shapes that let air and water slide past easily are called streamlined — think of a dolphin, a rocket, or a fast car.

Drag is not the same as weight. A feather and a stone can be dropped together, but the feather floats down slowly — not because it weighs almost nothing, but because it is wide and flat, so the air pushes back on it a lot. Change the shape and you change the drag, even if the weight stays the same.

To practise for a spacewalk, astronauts put on their suits and climb into an enormous swimming pool. Floating in the water feels a little like floating in space — but there is a catch. Water pushes back on every move, so if they wave an arm too fast, the water drags on it and slows them down. They learn to move slowly and smoothly, exactly the calm way they will need to move when they are really out among the stars.
