Floating and Sinking

Drop a cork in the bath and it bobs on top. Drop a stone and it plunges to the bottom. Both are being pulled down by their weight — so why does one stay up? The secret is that water does not just sit there. Water pushes back up on anything you put in it. That up-push is called upthrust.

Now it is a contest between two forces: weight pulling down, and upthrust pushing up.

  • If the up-push can balance the weight, the object floats.
  • If the weight wins, the object sinks.

Here is an object floating in a tank of water. Use the slider to make it heavier. While it is light, the upthrust (the up-arrow) matches its weight, so it rides high near the surface. Pile on more weight and, at some point, the down-arrow beats the up-arrow — the water cannot hold it up any more, and it sinks to the bottom.

Shape is the clever trick

Here is a puzzle: a solid ball of metal sinks like a stone, yet enormous metal ships float across the ocean. How? It is all about shape.

A ball is squeezed into a tiny space, so it only shoves a little water aside. A boat is spread out wide and hollow, so it pushes aside a huge amount of water — and the more water it shoves out of the way, the harder the water pushes back up. Spread out into a boat shape, the same metal gets enough upthrust to float.

Floating is not simply about being light or heavy. A giant tree trunk is very heavy indeed, yet it floats, while a tiny metal screw sinks at once. What matters is the contest between the object's weight and the upthrust its shape can gather — not its weight on its own.

Sea water has salt dissolved in it, which makes it heavier than the plain water from your tap. Heavier water gives a stronger upthrust — a bigger up-push for the same you. That is why it is so much easier to float in the salty sea than in a freshwater lake. In one lake, the Dead Sea, the water is so salty that you bob on the top like a cork and can read a book without even trying to swim.
