Recording Results

You did the test and got your numbers — now don't lose them! A scientist records results neatly, so they can be checked, shared, and understood. The best first home for your numbers is a table: one row per thing you tried, with its measurement beside it.

A table like this keeps everything tidy. For a plant-growing test you might write:

  • Plant A — 3 cm
  • Plant B — 5 cm
  • Plant C — 4 cm
  • Plant D — 2 cm

A table is tidy, but a picture is powerful. Turn the numbers into a bar chart — one bar per thing, and the taller the bar, the bigger the number. Suddenly the tallest and shortest jump straight out at you, without reading a single digit. For results that go together — like height as time passes — a line graph works too, and if you're looking for a link between two measurements, a scatter plot is the tool.

Here are the four plants as bars. Change Plant D and watch its bar grow or shrink. When D is the tallest bar, you can spot it in an instant — that's why scientists draw charts. Reading the answer back off the picture is just as important as making it.
