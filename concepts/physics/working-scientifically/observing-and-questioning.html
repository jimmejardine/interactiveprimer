Observing and Asking Questions

A scientist starts by noticing. You watch something happen and describe it in plain words: the ice is melting, the ball bounced twice, this rock is heavier than that one. Good noticing is the seed of every discovery.

Then you ask a question — but the best science questions are ones you can actually test. "Which paper aeroplane flies furthest?" is a great question: you can throw them and find out. Once you have a question, you can make a prediction — your best guess at the answer — and then test it.

Sorting: a scientist's superpower

Noticing often means sorting things into groups by one property — their size, their shape, their colour, whether they float. Sorting forces you to look at exactly one thing at a time, which is a habit you'll use again and again.
