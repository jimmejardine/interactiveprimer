Measuring in Science

"It's a bit warm" and "it's a bit far" are fine for chatting — but a scientist wants a number. Measuring turns a vague feeling into something you can write down, compare, and trust. The trick is to grab the right tool and read its scale carefully.

  • Measure length (how long) with a ruler or tape — in centimetres or metres.
  • Measure mass (how heavy) with a balance or scales — in grams or kilograms.
  • Measure time (how long it takes) with a timer or stopwatch — in seconds.
  • Measure temperature (how hot or cold) with a thermometer — in degrees.

Every tool has a scale — a row of marks with numbers. Reading a scale takes care: look straight on, find the mark the pointer or liquid reaches, and say the number with its unit. If you're rusty, the maths pages on reading scales and length, mass and capacity show you exactly how.
