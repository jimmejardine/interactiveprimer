Fair Tests

Imagine you want to know: does a car roll further from a higher ramp? To find a real answer, your test has to be fair. A fair test has a simple recipe:

  • Change one thing — here, the height of the ramp.
  • Measure one thing — how far the car rolls.
  • Keep everything else the same — the same car, the same floor, the same push.

That way, if the car goes further, you know it was the ramp height that did it — because nothing else was allowed to change.

Raise and lower the ramp. Only one thing changes — the height — and you watch one thing: how far the car rolls (the dashed line). The car and the floor stay exactly the same every single time, so the test is fair.

If you change two things at once, the test is spoiled. Suppose you make the ramp higher and swap to a heavier car. Now the car rolls a different distance — but was it the ramp or the car? You can't tell! To keep it fair, change one thing at a time and leave everything else alone.

When doctors check if a new medicine works, they give it to one group of people and give a look-alike pill with nothing in it to another group — keeping everything else the same: same ages, same illness, same care. That way the only difference is the medicine, so they can trust the result. It's your ramp-and-car fair test, grown up.
