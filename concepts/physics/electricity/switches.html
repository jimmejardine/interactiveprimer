Switches

Every day you make electricity do exactly what you want without ever touching a wire. You press a light switch on the wall, click a torch, or tap the button on a game controller. The magic part in every one of these is a switch.

A switch is really very simple: it is a little gap in the loop that you can open and close. Remember, charge can only flow when the loop is a complete circle. So the switch is like a drawbridge across the road — lower it and the traffic flows, lift it and everything stops.

Have a go. When the switch is Closed the little lever drops down and joins the two ends of the wire — the loop is complete, so the bulb lights. When it is Open the lever lifts up and leaves a gap — the loop is broken, no charge can get round, and the bulb goes dark. That is how you turn a light on and off: not by taking the bulb out, but simply by making or breaking the loop.

Switches are everywhere

  • The light switch on your wall opens or closes the loop to the ceiling light.
  • A torch button closes the loop between the batteries and the bulb.
  • A doorbell button is a switch that only closes the loop while you press it.

In every case the same thing is happening: a gap in the loop is being closed to let charge flow, or opened to stop it.

Sometimes a loop has two switches in it, one after the other. For the bulb to light, both switches must be closed at the same time — because the charge has to travel all the way round, and a single open switch anywhere is enough to break the loop. One open gap stops everything, no matter how many other switches are closed.

Have you ever felt a toaster pop, or seen a kettle click off by itself when the water boils? Those hide a clever switch that opens on its own when things get too hot.

Inside is a strip made of two metals stuck together that bend by different amounts as they warm up. When it gets hot the strip curls, lifts away, and opens the gap in the loop — turning the heat off before anything burns. No one has to remember to switch it off; the circuit looks after itself.
