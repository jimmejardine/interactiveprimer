Simple Circuits

Open up a torch and you will find the whole of electricity in miniature: a battery, a tiny bulb, and a switch, joined by strips of metal. Slide the switch and the bulb lights. Slide it back and it goes dark. What is really going on?

The battery pushes charge around the metal, and when the charge reaches the bulb it makes it glow. But the charge can only flow if it has a complete loop to travel round — out of one end of the battery, through the bulb, and back into the other end. That unbroken loop is called a circuit.

Try it yourself. When the switch is Closed the loop is complete, so charge flows all the way round and the bulb lights. When it is Open there is a gap — the loop is broken, no charge can flow, and the bulb stays dark. Nothing else changes: the same battery, the same bulb. The only thing that matters is whether the loop is complete.

The three jobs in a circuit

  • The battery is the pump — it pushes the charge around the loop.
  • The bulb is the worker — the charge flowing through it makes it glow.
  • The wires are the road — metal, so charge slides through them easily.

Break the road anywhere — a gap, a snapped wire, an open switch — and everything stops at once.

A very common mistake is to think a bulb will light with just one wire from the battery. It won't! Charge needs a round trip — a wire going to the bulb and a wire coming back to the battery. One wire is a dead end; only a complete loop lights the bulb.

The charge itself actually drifts round the wire slower than a snail — slower than you walk! So how can the bulb light up the very moment you close the switch, even on a long wire?

Because the wire is already full of charge everywhere, all the time — like a hose already full of water. The moment you complete the loop, every bit of charge starts to move together at once, so the charge already sitting inside the bulb gets going immediately. You don't wait for charge to travel from the battery; you just tell the whole loopful of charge to go.
