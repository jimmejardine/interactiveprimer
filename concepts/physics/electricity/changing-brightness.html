Changing the Brightness

A bulb does not have to shine at just one strength. Sometimes you want a bright, dazzling light, and sometimes a soft, gentle glow. With a simple circuit you can decide — and there are two easy ways to do it.

It all comes down to the push. The cells (the batteries) do the pushing, and the bulbs use it up. Give the charge a bigger push and each bulb burns brighter. Ask the same push to light more bulbs at once and each one has to make do with a smaller share.

Play with the two controls. Add more cells and watch the light blaze brighter — more cells push the charge harder. Then add more bulbs to the same loop and watch them all go dimmer — now the one push has to be shared out, so each bulb gets less. More push means brighter; more sharing means dimmer.

Two ways to change the glow

  • More cells → a stronger push → brighter bulbs.
  • More bulbs in one loop → the push is shared → each one is dimmer.

So a torch with two batteries shines brighter than a torch with one — and a string of many bulbs all sharing a single battery would glow only faintly.

There is a limit to piling on cells. Too much push sends too much charge racing through the thin wire inside the bulb — it gets so hot that it snaps, and the bulb "blows" and stops working for good. Brighter and brighter is not always better: every bulb has a push it is built for.

The dimmer knob on a wall — the one that slides a room from bright to cosy — is doing something clever. It does not remove any cells. Instead it quietly makes it harder for the charge to get through, squeezing the push down like a thumb over a hose.

A smaller push means a softer glow. Turn the knob the other way, the squeeze eases off, the push returns, and the room fills with light again — all without ever changing the battery.
