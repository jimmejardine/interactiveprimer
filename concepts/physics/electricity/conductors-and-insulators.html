Conductors and Insulators

Pick up a metal spoon and a plastic ruler. They both feel solid, but electricity treats them completely differently. Charge can stream easily through some materials and cannot get through others at all.

  • A conductor lets electricity flow through it. Most metals — copper, iron, steel, gold — are good conductors.
  • An insulator blocks electricity. Plastic, wood, rubber and glass are insulators.

This circuit has a gap in it, and you can drop different objects into the gap to test them. If the object is a conductor, charge flows straight through it, the loop is complete, and the bulb lights. If it is an insulator, it blocks the charge, the loop stays broken, and the bulb stays dark. Try each one and watch what happens.

Why wires are built the way they are

Look closely at any wire in your home and you will see it is made of two materials at once. In the middle runs metal — a conductor — so the charge has an easy road to travel along. Wrapped around the outside is a coat of plastic — an insulator — so the electricity cannot leak out into your hand. Conductor on the inside to carry the charge, insulator on the outside to keep it safely in: the perfect team.

Because your body is a bit of a conductor, electricity can flow through you too — and a strong current can hurt you badly. That is exactly why the plug and wire you hold are wrapped in plastic and rubber. Never poke metal things into a socket: the socket has real, powerful electricity behind it, not the gentle push of a torch battery.

Is water a conductor or an insulator? Completely pure water is actually a rather poor conductor. But the water in a puddle, a bath, or a rainstorm is never pure — it is full of dissolved bits from soap, salt and dirt, and those turn it into a decent conductor.

That is why we are told never to use electrical things with wet hands or near the bath. The electricity does not care that it is water — it just finds an easy path and takes it.
