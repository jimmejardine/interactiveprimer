Isaac Newton

Meet Isaac Newton (1642–1727): a premature baby so tiny that his mother said he could have fitted inside a quart pot, born on Christmas morning to a father already three months dead, in a stone farmhouse in a Lincolnshire hamlet nobody had heard of. The two women attending the birth were sent to fetch supplies and reportedly dawdled, certain the child would be gone before they got back. He lived to eighty-four, and by the time he died he was the most famous scientist on Earth — arguably the most famous who has ever lived. Not a bad glow-up.

His childhood explains a lot about the man. When Isaac was three, his mother remarried a wealthy elderly clergyman who wanted the wife but not the toddler, so the boy was left behind at Woolsthorpe with his grandmother. Years later, as a student, Newton kept a private notebook of his sins; one entry confesses to "threatening my father and mother Smith to burn them and the house over them." He grew into a solitary, secretive, thin-skinned genius who trusted almost no one, forgave almost nothing — and thought harder, and longer, than perhaps anyone before or since. Asked late in life how he had discovered the law of gravitation, he answered simply: "By thinking on it continually."

Yes, there was an apple. No, it did not bonk him on the head — we'll get to what actually happened, because the documented story is better than the cartoon. But first: the plague.

The miracle years: 1665–1667

In the summer of 1665 the bubonic plague swept out of London and across England, killing roughly a quarter of London's population. Cambridge University did the only sensible thing available in a world without medicine: it shut its gates and sent everyone home. Newton, a 22-year-old student who had so far distinguished himself mainly by filling notebooks with questions the curriculum didn't answer, packed up and rode back to his mother's farm at Woolsthorpe.

What happened next has no parallel in the history of science. Alone in the countryside, with no professors, no library, and nothing but time, Newton spent about eighteen months inventing the future. In roughly the same stretch that a modern student spends on one degree, he:

  • worked out the generalised binomial theorem, then built on it a complete new mathematics of change — his "method of fluxions," which the world now calls calculus;
  • discovered, with a prism and a hole in a window shutter, that white light is not pure but a mixture of every colour, overturning two thousand years of optics;
  • began comparing the fall of an apple with the orbit of the Moon, and found that a single pull, weakening with the square of the distance, could account for both — the seed of universal gravity.

Historians call it the annus mirabilis — the miracle year (it was really closer to two). Newton himself, looking back as an old man, put it plainly: "All this was in the two plague years of 1665 and 1666, for in those days I was in the prime of my age for invention, and minded mathematics and philosophy more than at any time since." He was not boasting. He was, if anything, underselling it.

And then — this is the strangest part — he told almost nobody. The plague passed, Cambridge reopened, and Newton went back with the foundations of modern mathematics and physics in his trunk and mostly kept them there.

The prism in the darkened room

Picture the experiment that made his name. Newton buys a glass prism at Stourbridge Fair — a cheap toy, sold for making pretty rainbow patterns. Back in his rooms he shutters the windows, bores a small hole in one shutter, and lets a single beam of sunlight cross the dark room to the far wall, twenty-two feet away. The prism goes into the beam — and the neat round spot of sunlight smears into an oblong band of colour: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet.

Everyone before him had assumed the prism stained the light — that glass somehow dirtied pure white sunlight into colours. Newton's genius was the follow-up, the experiment he called his experimentum crucis, the "crucial experiment." He let just one colour of the band — say, the red — pass through a slit and into a second prism. If prisms stain light, the red should change again. It didn't. Red stayed red; blue stayed blue. The colours were not being added by the glass — they were being sorted. White light is all the colours travelling together, and the prism merely bends each by a different amount and fans them apart.

His curiosity about light had a reckless edge. In the same notebooks he records staring at the Sun in a mirror until bright spots haunted his vision for days — and, more alarmingly, sliding a bodkin (a blunt, flat-ended needle) into his eye socket, "betwixt my eye and the bone, as near to the backside of my eye as I could," and pressing on his own eyeball to see what circles of colour appeared. He was testing whether colour lives in the light or in the eye. Genius, yes. A role model for laboratory safety, absolutely not — do not, under any circumstances, try this one at home.

The optics work had one immediate, practical payoff. Telescopes of the day used lenses, and lenses split colours slightly (that same sorting effect), fringing every star with rainbow blur. Newton reasoned that a curved mirror would focus all colours identically — so he built the first working reflecting telescope, grinding the mirror himself with tools he had also made himself. It was barely six inches long and magnified forty times. When the Royal Society in London saw it in 1672, they elected him a Fellow almost on the spot. Nearly every large telescope on Earth — and in space — is a descendant of that little tube.

Then came the reviews. Robert Hooke, the Royal Society's brilliant, combative curator of experiments — who had his own theory of light — criticised Newton's paper. Newton took it the way he took all criticism: as an act of war. After several rounds of increasingly icy correspondence he essentially withdrew from public science for a decade, muttering that natural philosophy was "an impertinently litigious lady." In one letter to Hooke he wrote the most quoted sentence of his life: "If I have seen further it is by standing on the shoulders of giants." It reads as beautiful humility — and it may be. But the phrase was a well-worn proverb, the letter sits inside a prickly dispute about who owed what to whom, and Hooke was a short man with a pronounced stoop. Many historians have wondered whether Newton's famous humility carried a concealed barb: whoever I'm standing on, Robert, it isn't you.

Halley knocks on the door

In January 1684, three members of the Royal Society — Christopher Wren, Robert Hooke and the astronomer Edmond Halley (of comet fame) — fell into a coffee-house argument. Everyone suspected that the Sun's pull on the planets weakened with the square of the distance. But could anyone prove that such a force produces exactly the elliptical orbits that Kepler had read out of the observations? Hooke claimed he could. Wren, unconvinced, offered a prize — a book worth forty shillings — for a demonstration within two months. Nobody collected.

That August, Halley travelled to Cambridge and put the question to the reclusive Lucasian Professor of Mathematics. What path would a planet follow under an inverse-square force? An ellipse, said Newton, instantly. A thrilled, astonished Halley asked how he knew. "I have calculated it." He had solved the outstanding problem of the age, years earlier, and filed it in a drawer. When he couldn't find the papers, he promised to redo the work and send it on.

What Halley got, three months later, was a nine-page treatise that answered the question — and then Newton simply kept going. For about eighteen months he worked with a ferocity that frightened his few observers: forgetting meals, forgetting sleep, pacing his garden and bolting inside to write standing up at his desk when an idea struck. The nine pages swelled into the Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica — the Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy — published in 1687 and still, pound for pound, the most consequential science book ever printed.

The Principia did something no book had done before: it unified the heavens and the Earth under one set of rules. The same three laws of motion and one law of gravitation explained a thrown stone, the tides, the wobble of the Earth's axis, the paths of comets and the orbit of the Moon. Before Newton, the sky was one kind of physics and the ground another. After him, there was just physics.

One more detail deserves remembering: the Principia nearly didn't appear. The Royal Society had emptied its publishing budget on a lavish flop called The History of Fishes, so Halley — not a rich man — paid for the printing of the greatest science book in history out of his own pocket, and did the proofreading too. The Society, unable to pay Halley's own salary that year, offered to settle in unsold copies of the fish book.

What he actually built

Strip away the legends and Newton's ideas are load-bearing walls of the modern world — and of this Primer. His first law says that a moving thing keeps moving, straight and steady, unless a force interferes — see Newton's first law. His second law says exactly how much a force changes motion, and it still fits on a sticky note:

F = ma

— force equals mass times acceleration (the full story is at Newton's second law). His third law — every action has an equal and opposite reaction — is why rockets fly and why the Earth tugs up on you exactly as hard as you press down on it (Newton's third law). And his law of universal gravitation says every scrap of matter in the universe pulls on every other, with a strength that fades with the square of the distance — the idea behind the whole story of gravity, from falling apples to the orbits of the outer planets.

Underneath all of it sits the mathematics he invented to make it possible: calculus, the mathematics of change — of speeds that vary, curves that bend, and quantities that accumulate. When NASA steers a probe past Jupiter, it is doing Newton: his laws, solved with his calculus. Physics needed almost nothing more for over two hundred years, until Einstein showed where the edges of Newton's picture lay — and even Einstein's equations settle back into Newton's whenever speeds are modest and gravity is gentle. Your GPS uses both.

Beyond the headline laws, he gave us the reflecting telescope, the theory of colour, the generalised binomial theorem, a workhorse trick for solving equations that programmers still ship as "Newton's method," a first calculation of the speed of sound, and the proof that a spinning, gravitating Earth should bulge at the equator (it does).

He invented calculus and then ghosted everyone

To do his physics, Newton needed a new kind of maths for things that change — so he just… invented calculus. Then he sat on it for decades without properly publishing it, because he hated criticism and couldn't be bothered with the drama. He circulated manuscripts among a few trusted mathematicians, dropped hints, and once even buried his claim to the method inside a letter as an anagram — a scrambled Latin sentence, so he could later prove he'd known it first without actually telling anyone what it was.

Which was awkward, because over in Germany a man named Leibniz invented calculus too, completely independently, and published first — in 1684 — with much nicer notation. (The \dfrac{dy}{dx} and \int symbols you use today are Leibniz's; Newton's dot-notation \dot{x} survives mainly in physics.) Cue the pettiest, longest-running beef in the history of mathematics — a priority war that poisoned relations between British and Continental mathematics for a century. More on the feud below, and on Leibniz's own page.

Two myths cling to Newton, and both are worth peeling apart from the truth.

Myth one: an apple fell on his head and — bonk — gravity. No falling fruit struck anyone, and gravity as "things fall down" needed no discovering. The documented version comes from Newton's friend William Stukeley, who wrote down a conversation the two had over tea in a London garden in 1726, the year before Newton died. Sitting under apple trees, the old man said the idea of gravitation had come to him in just such a setting at Woolsthorpe, "occasioned by the fall of an apple, as he sat in a contemplative mood." The question the apple posed was sharper than the cartoon: why does it always fall perpendicularly — straight towards the Earth's centre? And if that pull reaches the top of the tallest tree undiminished, how high does it reach? To the Moon? Then his brain-melting answer: the Moon is falling — it just also moves sideways so fast that it keeps missing the Earth, forever. The apple is real, and documented; the bonk on the head was added later to make a better cartoon.

Myth two: Newton "published calculus in 1665." He devised his method of fluxions in the plague years — but he published essentially none of it for decades. The Principia of 1687 was deliberately written in the language of classical geometry, its calculus hidden under the floorboards; his first real account of fluxions appeared as an appendix to the Opticks in 1704 — nearly forty years after the discovery — and his fullest treatment only surfaced after his death. That long silence is precisely what made the priority war with Leibniz possible: by the time Newton's methods were public, Leibniz's independently invented version had been in print for twenty years. Sitting on your results, it turns out, has costs.

Second act: the crime-fighting civil servant

Here the story takes a turn no novelist would dare. In 1693 Newton suffered some kind of breakdown — sleepless, paranoid, firing off wild accusatory letters to friends and then apologising, baffled, months later. Whatever its cause (grief, overwork and years of inhaling mercury fumes over alchemical crucibles have all been suggested — analysis of his surviving hair found mercury levels well above normal), when he recovered, something had changed. He was done with Cambridge. In 1696 his friends secured him a comfortable sinecure in London: Warden of the Royal Mint, a job the Chancellor assured him needed "not more attendance than you can spare."

Newton, constitutionally incapable of doing anything at half-throttle, treated the cushy job description as a clerical error. England's silver coinage was in crisis — clipped, shaved, and counterfeited to the point where perhaps one coin in ten was fake — and the country was attempting the Great Recoinage: melting down and re-minting the entire currency. Newton mastered the metallurgy, reorganised the production lines, and drove the Mint's output to several times its old rate. By 1700 he was Master of the Mint, a post he held until his death.

And then there was the detective work. Counterfeiting was high treason — a hanging offence — and prosecuting it was, absurdly, part of the Warden's job. Newton took to it with the thoroughness he'd once aimed at planetary orbits: he had himself commissioned as a magistrate, ran networks of informers, and went in person — sometimes, the stories say, in disguise — into taverns and prisons to take depositions from coiners, fences and their girlfriends. He conducted more than a hundred interrogations, and the man who had spent thirty years refusing to answer his critics' letters proved to be a relentless, detail-perfect prosecutor.

In 1703, the year his old enemy Hooke died, Newton was elected President of the Royal Society, and ran it — autocratically, effectively — for the last twenty-four years of his life. In 1705 Queen Anne knighted him: Sir Isaac Newton, the first scientist honoured for his science (well — for his science and his politics). The farm boy from Woolsthorpe had become the establishment.

Newton's greatest case reads like a thriller. William Chaloner was the most audacious counterfeiter in England — a con man who had forged tens of thousands of coins, lectured Parliament (twice!) on how to fix the Mint's security, and even petitioned to be put in charge of it, all while running coining operations of his own. He made the fatal mistake of publicly accusing Newton's Mint of incompetence and corruption.

Newton came after him the way he came after every problem: totally. When a first prosecution collapsed (Chaloner had friends, and witnesses conveniently vanished), Newton did not shrug and move on — he spent more than a year quietly rebuilding the case, interviewing accomplices, planting informers in Newgate prison, and cross-checking testimony like a man balancing an equation. In March 1699 Chaloner was convicted of high treason and hanged at Tyburn. His last letter begged Newton for mercy — "O dear Sir nobody can save me but you O God my God I shall be murdered unless you save me." Newton, as far as the records show, did not reply. He was a magnificent scientist; he was not a soft touch.

The dark side of the giant

Be honest about your heroes. Newton in power was Newton unchecked, and two episodes stain the portrait.

The Leibniz war. When the priority dispute over calculus turned official, Leibniz appealed to the Royal Society for a fair hearing. The Society duly appointed an "impartial" committee — hand-picked by its president, Isaac Newton — whose report, the Commercium Epistolicum (1712), found firmly for Newton. Unsurprising, since Newton had anonymously written much of it himself, and then, for good measure, anonymously reviewed his own report — favourably. Leibniz died in 1716, his reputation under a cloud; Newton reportedly boasted that he had "broke his heart." The verdict of history, for what it's worth: both men invented calculus, independently — Newton first in private, Leibniz first in print — and the war benefited no one. British mathematics, loyally boycotting Leibniz's superior notation for a century, arguably punished itself worst of all.

The erasing of Hooke. Newton waited until Hooke was dead to publish the Opticks (1704), the great book of his light experiments, rather than give his old critic the satisfaction of reviewing it. As President he moved the Royal Society to new premises, and in the move the Society's only portrait of Hooke vanished — carelessness or curation, no one can prove, but the effect is stark: no verified portrait of one of England's greatest experimenters survives anywhere. Hooke had genuinely glimpsed pieces of the gravitation story before Newton and said so, loudly; he lacked the mathematics to prove any of it, and Newton repaid the needling by scrubbing him from the record wherever the record could be reached. Give the man his due: Newton saw further than everyone. He just also made sure some of the shoulders got forgotten.

In 1936 a trunk of Newton's private papers — kept from public view for two centuries — was auctioned at Sotheby's, and the economist John Maynard Keynes bought a large share of them. What he read astonished him. Alongside the physics were something like a million words on alchemy — recipes, coded experiment logs, obsessive pursuit of the philosopher's stone that was supposed to turn base metal into gold — and at least as much again on secret, forbidden theology. Keynes announced his verdict in a famous lecture: "Newton was not the first of the age of reason. He was the last of the magicians."

The theology was genuinely dangerous. Newton had convinced himself, through decades of minute biblical scholarship, that the doctrine of the Trinity was a fourth-century corruption — which made him a heretic by the standards of his day, and made his own position exquisitely awkward: he was a fellow of Trinity College, in an age when fellows were required to take holy orders. He obtained a special royal dispensation excusing him, kept his beliefs encrypted in private notebooks, and took the secret to his grave (declining, on his deathbed, the final sacrament of the Church). He also computed — from the prophecies of Daniel, with characteristic rigour — that the world could not end before AD 2060. So we have that going for us.

Was the "magic" a waste? Not entirely. The alchemy was, in its way, careful experimental chemistry a century before chemistry existed, and some historians think it kept Newton comfortable with an idea his mechanical-minded contemporaries choked on: that one body might act on another across empty space, with nothing visible in between. Which is to say — gravity. The magician and the physicist may have needed each other.

A life in ten lines

  • 1642 — born prematurely at Woolsthorpe, Lincolnshire, on Christmas Day (4 January 1643 by our modern calendar), his father already dead.
  • 1661 — enters Trinity College, Cambridge, earning his keep partly as a servant to wealthier students.
  • 1665–67 — the plague years at Woolsthorpe: calculus, the theory of colours, and the seed of universal gravitation, in about eighteen months.
  • 1669 — Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at 26, when his teacher Isaac Barrow steps aside for him.
  • 1672 — the reflecting telescope wins him the Royal Society; the first optics paper wins him the feud with Hooke.
  • 1684 — Halley's visit: "I have calculated it."
  • 1687 — the Principia, printed at Halley's personal expense.
  • 1696–1727 — Warden, then Master, of the Royal Mint; scourge of counterfeiters.
  • 1703–1727 — President of the Royal Society; knighted 1705; Opticks published 1704, once Hooke was safely dead.
  • 1727 — dies in London; buried in Westminster Abbey with a pomp normally reserved for kings.

Voltaire, who attended the funeral, marvelled that England had buried a mathematician "like a king who had done good to his subjects." Alexander Pope supplied the epitaph the age wanted: "Nature and Nature's laws lay hid in night: God said, Let Newton be! and all was light." Newton's own last recorded self-assessment is quieter, and better: "I do not know what I may appear to the world, but to myself I seem to have been only like a boy playing on the sea-shore, and diverting myself in now and then finding a smoother pebble or a prettier shell than ordinary, whilst the great ocean of truth lay all undiscovered before me."
