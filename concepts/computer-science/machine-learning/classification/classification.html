Classification

Imagine two weather questions. "How hot will it be tomorrow?" wants a number — maybe 24°C, maybe 19°C. "Will it rain tomorrow?" wants a label — yes or no. Both questions are predictions about the future, and both can be learned from years of past weather data, but they are fundamentally different jobs.

Regression answers the first kind of question: it predicts a number that can slide smoothly up or down — a price, a temperature, a height. Classification answers the second kind: it predicts a category from a fixed menu of choices — spam or not spam, cat or dog, benign or malignant. Same idea of "learn from labelled examples," completely different kind of output.

Both are flavours of supervised learning: you show the model thousands of past examples where you already know the right answer — past emails already marked "spam" or "not spam" by a human, past scans already confirmed "malignant" or "benign" by a doctor — and the model searches for a pattern that connects the inputs to those known answers. The difference between regression and classification is entirely about the shape of that answer: a number that can be anywhere on a scale, versus a label from a short, fixed list.

Sorting jobs into the right bucket

A good habit whenever you meet a new prediction task is to ask: "is the answer a sliding number, or a pick-one-from-a-list category?" Try it on a few real jobs:

  • Predicting tomorrow's exact temperature in degrees → a number that can be 19.4°C or 19.5°C or anything in between → regression.
  • Predicting whether it will rain tomorrow → one of two labels, "rain" or "no rain" → classification.
  • Predicting a house's sale price → a number, in dollars → regression.
  • Predicting whether a tumour is malignant or benign → one of two labels → classification.

Notice the tell: if it makes sense to ask "by how much did I miss?", it's regression (missing a temperature by 2°C is a small error, missing by 20°C is a big one). If the answer can only ever be "right label" or "wrong label" — with no in-between — it's classification.

Two flavours of classification: binary and multi-class

Not every classification problem has the same number of buckets. When there are exactly two possible categories, we call it binary classification. A spam filter is the classic example: every incoming email gets sorted into just one of two piles, spam or not spam. There is no third option.

When there are three or more categories, we call it multi-class classification. Recognising handwritten digits is a favourite training ground for this: a small square image of a scrawled digit goes in, and the model must pick exactly one answer out of ten possibilities — the digits 0 through 9. Sorting photos into "cat," "dog," or "bird" is multi-class with three buckets; diagnosing which of several diseases a patient has might be multi-class with a dozen.

The core idea — learn from labelled examples, then assign new examples to a category — stays the same whether there are 2 buckets or 200. Only the size of the menu changes. One of the most famous multi-class datasets in machine learning, called MNIST, contains 70,000 tiny grayscale images of handwritten digits — the very "which of 10 digits" task above — and for decades it has been the classic first project for anyone learning to build a classifier.

Worked example: sort five real jobs

Let's practise the sorting habit on a small batch of real prediction jobs, reasoning through each one out loud the way a data scientist would on day one of a new project:

  • "How many centimetres of rain will fall this week?" — the answer can be 0.0 cm, 3.2 cm, 15.7 cm, anything in between. It slides continuously, so this is regression.
  • "Will there be a flood warning this week?" — the answer is one of a fixed handful of labels: "warning" or "no warning." There is no in-between reading, so this is binary classification.
  • "What will this used car sell for?" — dollars and cents, sliding smoothly from nothing up to whatever the market will bear: regression.
  • "Is this X-ray showing a fracture, and if so, of which of the 27 bones in the hand?" — a fixed menu of 28 labels (27 bones, or "no fracture"). Even though there are lots of options, it's still classification — specifically, multi-class.
  • "How many days until this library book is due back?" — a count that could be 1, 2, 3, … any whole number: regression again (some problems that "feel" like counting are still regression, because the answer slides rather than picking from a closed menu).

Notice the pattern holds even when the numbers involved get big or the categories get numerous — what matters is whether the output can take any value along a scale, or must be exactly one label from a fixed, known list.

Draw the dividing line

So how does a classifier actually make its choice? It doesn't wave a magic wand — it draws a boundary through the space of possible inputs and checks which side a new example lands on. Two classes are scattered below as coloured points. Rotate and shift the boundary line to separate them as cleanly as possible; the readout counts how many points it gets right. A line that puts every blue point on one side and every orange point on the other is a perfect classifier for this data.

Here the boundary happens to be a straight line, but it doesn't have to be — curved, wiggly, even broken-up boundaries are all fair game once the data won't separate so neatly. And a boundary drawn by hand, like the one you just dragged, is a blunt yes/no tool. Real classifiers usually do something a little richer: they compute how far a point sits from the boundary and turn that into a confidence score, which is exactly where the sigmoid function and the decision boundary pick up the story.

Notice, too, that a point sitting right on the boundary is the hardest kind of example — the model genuinely can't tell which side it belongs to. A point sitting far from the boundary, deep inside its own colour's territory, is an easy call. That distance-from-the-boundary idea — not just "which side," but "how far into which side" — is exactly what turns a blunt yes/no rule into a graded confidence score, and it is why classification and probability turn out to be so closely linked.

Two spam filters both flag the same email as "spam." Filter A is 51% sure. Filter B is 99% sure. They agree on the final label — but they are not saying the same thing at all. Filter A is barely more convinced than a coin flip; a tiny nudge in the email's wording could easily have flipped its answer. Filter B is nearly certain.

A classifier that only reports the final label throws this difference away. That's why good classifiers report a probability or confidence score alongside the label — a number between 0 and 1 that says how sure the model is, not just which side of the boundary it landed on. Squashing a raw internal score into that well-behaved 0-to-1 range is exactly the job of the sigmoid function.

Suppose only 1 in every 100 emails is actually spam — the categories are wildly unbalanced. Now imagine a "classifier" so lazy it never even looks at the email: it just guesses "not spam" every single time. How often is it right?

99% of the time — because 99 out of 100 emails really were "not spam" anyway. That sounds like a fantastic classifier, but it is completely useless: it will never, ever catch a piece of spam. This is the trap of unbalanced categories, and it's why judging a classifier by "accuracy" alone can be dangerously misleading. Telling a genuinely skilled classifier apart from a lazy one that got lucky on the numbers needs sharper tools — the evaluation metrics built specifically for this.

Classification isn't a laboratory curiosity — it quietly runs in the background of ordinary life. Your inbox's spam filter sorts every arriving email into "spam" or "not spam" before you ever see it. Hospitals use diagnosis-support tools that classify a scan or a blood test as "likely healthy" or "needs a closer look." Social media platforms run content-moderation classifiers around the clock, sorting millions of posts and images into "allowed" or "flagged for review."

And sometimes classifiers are built purely for laughs. The TV comedy Silicon Valley featured a (fictional, but working) app called "SeeFood" whose entire job was one single binary classification: given a photo, decide "hot dog" or "not hot dog." Silly premise, but under the hood it's the exact same kind of system as a medical scanner or a spam filter — just trained on a much tastier dataset.

A self-driving car's camera system runs a classifier many times a second, deciding "pedestrian" or "not pedestrian" on every patch of every frame. A bank's fraud system classifies each card swipe as "normal" or "suspicious" before you've even finished tapping your card. None of these systems are doing anything mysterious — every one of them is doing the same job as the sliders you just dragged: drawing a boundary, and checking which side a new example falls on.

The questions ahead

A hand-drawn line gives a blunt yes/no answer, but as you just saw, we usually want more: a probability — "87% likely spam" — and a principled way to draw the boundary rather than dragging it by eye. That calls for the sigmoid and logistic regression. And not every boundary is a straight line — but the straight-line case is where all the core ideas live, so that's where we start.
