Fitting a Line

Suppose you want to predict something before it happens: how much a house will sell for, given its size; what score a student will get, given how many hours they studied; how many ice-creams a shop will sell tomorrow, given the forecast temperature. In every one of these you have one measurable number going in (size, hours, temperature) and one number you want out (price, score, sales) — and if you plot enough past examples on a graph, the points often fall roughly along a straight line.

Linear regression is the technique for finding that straight line — the "best" line through the data — and then reading off a fresh prediction for a new input you haven't seen before. It's often called the "hello world" of machine learning: it's about as simple as a model gets, yet it already contains the whole skeleton of supervised learning — a model, an error to minimize, and a way to improve. Every fancier model you meet later — logistic regression, decision trees, neural networks — gets compared back to this one. If a much more complicated model can't beat a plain straight line on a given problem, that's a serious warning sign that the extra complexity isn't earning its keep.

It's also worth pausing on just how ordinary this technique is. It isn't buried in a research lab — spreadsheets have had a "trendline" button for decades, and it's exactly this. Every time a weather forecaster eyeballs a warming trend, or an app estimates your delivery time from distance, some version of this same straight-line idea is quietly at work underneath.

The setup

Formally, you're handed a batch of example pairs — an hours-studied number paired with the score that student actually got, or a temperature paired with that day's ice-cream sales. The input side of each pair (hours, temperature) is called a feature, and the output side (score, sales) is its label. With one feature x and one label y, the model is just a line, y = mx + b, and your job is to choose the slope m and intercept b that fit those examples best.

Why a straight line, of all the shapes you could pick? Partly because it's the simplest curve that isn't just a flat, useless constant — it needs only two numbers to describe completely — and partly because a huge number of real relationships genuinely look roughly straight over the range we care about, even if they curve a little at the extremes.

"Best" means the line with the smallest overall prediction error — summed up across every example, not just one. Turning that idea into an exact number is the job of the cost function, coming up next — for now, just trust that "best" can be measured, and that a computer can search for it.

Worked example: spotting the trend

A teacher jots down, for six students, how many hours they studied and the score they got:

(1\text{ hr}, 52), (2\text{ hr}, 58), (3\text{ hr}, 61), (4\text{ hr}, 68), (6\text{ hr}, 79), (8\text{ hr}, 89).

Even without doing any arithmetic, picture those six dots on a graph: as hours climb, scores climb too, roughly in step — an extra hour tends to buy a handful more points. That rough, mostly-straight climb is exactly the pattern linear regression is built to find. It won't pass through every point exactly (real data is never that tidy), but it will find the one line that threads through points like these most tightly overall.

You can even sanity-check a line by hand, roughly, before any fitting algorithm runs. Between the first pair and the last, the score rose by 89 - 52 = 37 points while hours rose by 8 - 1 = 7 — a climb of a bit over 5 points per hour. That rough "rise over run" guess for the slope, using nothing more than the two endpoints, is a handy reality check once a fitting algorithm hands you an actual answer — if the fitted slope came back wildly different, say negative, you'd know something had gone wrong.

Worked example: reading off a prediction

Now suppose someone has already done the fitting for you on a bigger version of that class's data, and found the best-fit line

\text{score} = 5.5 \times \text{hours} + 48.

Check it against a student who studied 4 hours: 5.5 \times 4 + 48 = 70 — close to the 68 that student actually scored, which is a good sign the line fits well.

Now here's the payoff. A brand-new student — one who wasn't in the original data at all — studied 9 hours. Plug it straight in: 5.5 \times 9 + 48 = 97.5. The line predicts a score of about 97.5, without that student ever having sat the exam yet. That's the entire reason we bothered fitting a line: not to describe the past, but to answer questions about the future.

Worked example: a real business decision

Linear regression isn't just for classrooms. An ice-cream shop's manager logs the average daily temperature and that day's sales all summer, fits a line, and gets

\text{scoops sold} = 12 \times \text{temperature (°C)} - 150.

Tomorrow's forecast is 30°C, so the manager predicts 12 \times 30 - 150 = 210 scoops, and orders stock accordingly. That's a real decision — how much ice-cream to buy, how many staff to schedule — resting on nothing more exotic than a straight line through some dots.

Only trust a fitted line for predicting within, or close to, the range of the original data. Wander far outside that range and the line keeps going in a straight path, whether or not the real world does.

Take that same ice-cream line, 12 \times \text{temperature} - 150, and plug in a chilly 5°C — nowhere near the summer days it was fitted on: 12 \times 5 - 150 = -90 scoops. Minus ninety scoops is nonsense; you can't un-sell ice-cream. The same trap can hit exam scores too — an oddly-shaped dataset can produce a fitted line that predicts a negative score for a student who studied zero hours, which is equally impossible. The line is only ever a good guide near the data it learned from; trusting it far outside that range is called extrapolating, and it's one of the most common ways machine-learning predictions go wrong. (You'll meet the general version of this idea — how well a model copes with situations it wasn't trained on — as generalization.)

No — and this trips up even careful people. A straight line fitting the ice-cream data well just means sales and temperature move together: as one goes up, so does the other, fairly reliably. That alone doesn't prove heat directly causes higher sales.

In this particular case it's very plausible that heat really is the cause — people crave something cold on a hot day. But imagine a different line: ice-cream sales and sunburn cases also rise together in summer. Ice-cream doesn't cause sunburn, and sunburn doesn't cause ice-cream cravings — a hidden third factor, sunny weather, drives both. A fitted line can tell you two things are correlated; it takes more careful reasoning (or a proper experiment) to establish that one truly causes the other.

A classic (and slightly silly) example economists like to trade: across countries, the number of storks nesting and the number of human births both used to rise together decade over decade — a beautifully straight line if you plotted them. Storks obviously don't deliver babies; both numbers were simply drifting upward alongside a country's general development. A good fit is a clue worth chasing, never a verdict on its own.

The name is a leftover from a rather charming 1880s puzzle. The scientist Francis Galton was measuring the heights of parents and their grown-up children, expecting very tall parents to have even taller children, generation after generation. Instead he found the opposite: children of unusually tall parents tended to be tall, but a little closer to the average height than their parents were. He called this pull back toward the average "regression toward mediocrity" — and when he fitted a straight line to describe it, the name regression stuck to the whole technique, even though almost nobody uses it to study heights any more.

Galton was a restless character — a half-cousin of Charles Darwin, he also pioneered weather maps, helped establish fingerprints as a way to identify people, and had a habit of trying to measure absolutely everything, including once ranking cities by how "attractive" the women in them seemed to him from a park bench, purely for his own amusement. The height study, almost an afterthought among his other obsessions, is the one that left the deepest mark on mathematics.

That makes linear regression well over 140 years old — practically ancient by computing standards — and yet it's still one of the most-used tools in real business analytics today. Not because nothing better exists, but because it's simple, fast, and its predictions are easy to explain to a human: "sales rise by 12 scoops per degree" is a sentence a shop manager can act on immediately, no data scientist required to translate it.

Fit it, then predict

Now try it yourself. Adjust the slope and intercept below to fit the eight points, watching the total error fall as the line settles into place. Then look at the pink query marker: once your line is good, it predicts a sensible y for an x that isn't in the data at all — exactly the trick from the worked examples above, just interactive.

Notice, too, how much easier a computer has it than you do here. You're nudging two sliders by eye, watching a single number labelled "error" and trying to make it smaller — which is exactly the problem gradient descent solves automatically, far faster and far more precisely, once we've defined that error number properly.

The plan from here

"Adjust until it fits" is fine by hand, but we need to make it precise and automatic. That takes three pieces: a model with tunable knobs, a cost that scores how wrong it is, and an algorithm — gradient descent — that turns the knobs to drive the cost down. We build them one at a time.
